Purpose

On average You browse through about 20 websites per day, however have you ever stopped to think about how it all came to be? If you never have, you are about to find out. This webpage will explore one man’s pursuit of how his desire of having an online reservoir of information led him to develop what we know today as a basic website. Our target audience is every user who is enabled to browse the internet, from the early ages of 10 right up to even 100. The target market may initially search the internet for some information, shop for an item online or may even be online to play video games. The majority might have an idea of the origin of the internet but may really really know. The minority will be intently searching for how the internet came to about.The users will be accessing the website via various digital devices.

Home page

Tim Berners Lee is the famous name behind the early development of the web. This British scientist working for an organization that is based in Geneva Switzerland, and includes a community of about 17000 scientists from 100 different countries, noticed a problem of how these scientists struggled to share their research amongst themselves. Tim sought to use three latest technologies at the time to tackle this problem.

  • HTML: HyperText Markup Language.
  • URI/URL: Uniform Resource Identifier. A website “address” that is used to identify each resource on the web.
  • HTTP: Hypertext Transfer Protocol. Allows for requests of data to be sent to and from resources across the web.

Tim drafted a proposal for the Hypertext project called the World Wide Web and made two consecutive proposals to his superiors. Following the rejection of the first in March 1989 the second finally got it’s approval the following year in May 1990. Tim immediately then started working on the first web server on an exclusive NeXT computer and the first web browser implementing his ideas from the Hypertext project. The very first address to the first website was http://info.cern.ch/. Tim wanted the web to have a global reach so he decided that the web would be completely free, so that anyone in the world could develop their own website to share information and more to any other person anywhere in the world. This is why we have plenty of websites today. Tim left CERN for Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) in 1994 where he engaged himself and a few others to establish a global community devoted to maintaining and enhancing the open web standards. The W3C as it is commonly referred to today sought to maintain the following standards;
  • “Decentralisation: No permission is needed from a central authority to post anything on the web, there is no central controlling node, and so no single point of failure … and no “kill switch”! This also implies freedom from indiscriminate censorship and surveillance.”
  • “Non-discrimination: If I pay to connect to the internet with a certain quality of service, and you pay to connect with that or a greater quality of service, then we can both communicate at the same level. This principle of equity is also known as Net Neutrality.”
  • “Bottom-up design: Instead of code being written and controlled by a small group of experts, it was developed in full view of everyone, encouraging maximum participation and experimentation.”
  • “Universality: For anyone to be able to publish anything on the web, all the computers involved have to speak the same languages to each other, no matter what different hardware people are using; where they live; or what cultural and political beliefs they have. In this way, the web breaks down silos while still allowing diversity to flourish.”
  • “Consensus: For universal standards to work, everyone had to agree to use them. Tim and others achieved this consensus by giving everyone a say in creating the standards, through a transparent, participatory process at W3C.”

The Consortium has a website that you may visit to learn more about its mission and other principles fundamental to its operation. The website address is https://www.w3.org/ . Additional topics you may expect to find on the site include internationalization, web security, privacy, code of ethics and professional conduct.

Building Blocks

The Hypertext Markup Language was and still is a fundamental component to Websites. HTML is comprised of many elements that can be used to render unique content on a browser. Each Element ascribes to the "Box-model concept" which simply means that each element is rendered as a rectangle on the web page. The rectangle consists of four layers; margin, border, padding and the actual content layer which is what is visible to the viewer. The other three layers are abstract however they play a key role in positioning and styling the rendered content.

Cascading Style Sheet is developed using HTML and is solely responsible for the presentation of almost all websites. An example of a presentation aspect of a web page would be background-color. A selector is used to reference the HTML element in question then a property of either one of the four layers we have mentioned above is invoked to which a value is assigned. As its name suggests this technology cascades the styling effect to all children elements.
